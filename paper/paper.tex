\begin{abstract}
This paper investigates the relationship between government system types
and democratic stability across 100+ countries from 2000-2023. Using
V-Dem democracy indicators, the analysis finds that the effect of
presidential versus parliamentary systems on democratic volatility is
conditional on economic development. Presidential systems exhibit lower
volatility in low-income contexts where executive autonomy aids crisis
response, while parliamentary systems show greater stability in
high-income countries where institutional constraints and party systems
are stronger. The crossover point occurs at approximately \$14,000 GDP
per capita (PPP). These findings advance understanding of institutional
resilience and have important implications for constitutional design in
democratizing states. (99 words)
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}

Democratic backsliding has emerged as one of the most significant
political phenomena of the 21st century (Bermeo 2016, 10--13; Levitsky
and Ziblatt 2018). From Hungary to Brazil, established democracies have
experienced erosion of democratic norms and institutions (Lührmann and
Lindberg 2019, 1105; Waldner and Lust 2018). Yet not all democracies are
equally vulnerable. Some demonstrate remarkable resilience while others
succumb to authoritarian pressures (Levitsky and Way 2023; Merkel and
Lührmann 2021). This variation raises a fundamental question about what
institutional configurations best protect democratic stability.

This paper investigates whether government system type (presidential,
parliamentary, or semi-presidential) affects democratic resilience, and
crucially, whether this relationship is conditional on a country's level
of economic development. The analysis challenges the conventional wisdom
pitting parliamentary against presidential systems, arguing that it
misses the conditional nature of institutional effects (Cheibub 2007,
140--43; Cheibub and Limongi 2002, 151). The central claim is that the
optimal system for democratic stability depends on the developmental
context.

Using data from the Varieties of Democracy (V-Dem) project covering
2000-2023 (Coppedge et al. 2011; Lindberg et al. 2014), democratic
stability is measured through volatility in liberal democracy scores.
The key finding is that presidential systems demonstrate lower
democratic volatility in low-income countries, while parliamentary
systems perform better in high-income contexts. The crossover point,
where neither system holds an advantage, occurs at approximately
\$14,000 GDP per capita (PPP).

These findings contribute to comparative politics scholarship by
demonstrating that institutional effects on democratic stability are not
universal but context-dependent (Brambor, Clark, and Golder 2006;
Hainmueller, Mummolo, and Xu 2019). The paper proceeds as follows.
Section 2 reviews the theoretical framework, Section 3 describes data
and methodology, Section 4 presents results, Section 5 illustrates
findings with case studies, Section 6 discusses causal interpretation,
and Section 7 concludes.

\section{Theoretical Framework}\label{theoretical-framework}

\subsection{Government Systems and Democratic
Stability}\label{government-systems-and-democratic-stability}

The debate over optimal constitutional design has long centered on the
relative merits of presidential versus parliamentary systems. Linz
(1990, 55--56) famously argued that presidential systems are prone to
democratic breakdown due to the rigidity of fixed terms, winner-take-all
elections, and potential for executive-legislative deadlock. In an
earlier unpublished work, Linz (1985, 6--7) emphasized the ``zero-sum''
character of presidential elections where ``the winner takes all'' and
losers must wait years for another chance---unlike parliamentary systems
where coalition flexibility allows parties to share power (Linz 1985,
5--6; see also Linz and Valenzuela 1994). Parliamentary systems, by
contrast, offer flexibility through votes of confidence and coalition
building (Horowitz 1990).

However, this conventional view has been challenged on both theoretical
and empirical grounds. Cheibub (2007, 140--43) showed that the
correlation between presidentialism and democratic instability may be
spurious, driven by the military legacies of Latin American countries
that adopted presidential systems. Cheibub and Limongi (2002, 151) found
that between 1946 and 1999, one in every 23 presidential regimes became
a dictatorship, while only one in every 58 parliamentary regimes did.
However, they argue this reflects selection effects rather than
institutional causation. Stepan and Skach (1993) found that among
post-communist transitions, parliamentary systems had higher survival
rates, but acknowledged significant regional confounding.

More recent scholarship emphasizes that institutional effects are
conditional on other factors (Mainwaring 1993; Hiroi and Omori 2009).
Fish (2006) demonstrates that legislative strength matters more than
executive structure, while Shugart and Carey (1992) highlights variation
within presidential systems based on presidential powers and electoral
rules. Maeda (2010) shows that the risk of democratic breakdown in
presidential systems is not initially different from parliamentary
systems but becomes significantly higher after approximately 10 years.

\subsection{Theoretical Mechanisms}\label{theoretical-mechanisms}

Three mechanisms are proposed through which the system type-stability
relationship varies with development.

\subsubsection{Crisis Response Capacity}\label{crisis-response-capacity}

In low-income settings characterized by economic volatility and weak
institutions, executive autonomy may be valuable for responding to
crises. Presidential systems concentrate decision-making authority,
potentially enabling faster responses to shocks (Valenzuela 1993). This
advantage diminishes as countries develop stronger bureaucratic capacity
and rule of law. Bolton and Thrower (2021) show that when legislative
capacity is low, executives can act more unilaterally. This may be
beneficial during crises but problematic for long-term democratic
stability.

\subsubsection{Institutional
Constraints}\label{institutional-constraints}

At higher development levels, horizontal constraints such as judicial
independence, legislative oversight, and media freedom become more
robust (Gibler and Randazzo 2011; Issacharoff 2014). These constraints,
which are more naturally embedded in parliamentary systems, become
increasingly important for preventing democratic erosion as countries
develop (Tusalem 2023). Bolton and Thrower (2016) demonstrate that
legislative capacity constrains executive unilateralism; this constraint
mechanism operates more effectively in wealthier countries with stronger
institutions.

\subsubsection{Party
Institutionalization}\label{party-institutionalization}

Parliamentary systems require strong, institutionalized parties to
function effectively (Mainwaring and Torcal 2006; Bizzarro et al. 2018).
In low-income countries with weak party systems, the parliamentary
model's dependence on stable coalitions may actually increase
instability (Randall and Svåsand 2002; Diskin, Diskin, and Hazan 2005).
As development progresses, party systems typically strengthen
(Yardımcı-Geyikçi 2015), enabling parliamentary systems to realize their
stability advantages. Cheibub, Przeworski, and Saiegh (2004, 140--43)
find that coalition governments emerge in presidential systems for
similar reasons as in parliamentary systems, but parliamentary
coalitions benefit from stronger institutional incentives for
maintenance (Chaisty, Cheeseman, and Power 2014).

\section{Data and Methods}\label{sec-data}

\subsection{Data Sources}\label{data-sources}

We combine four major cross-national datasets:

\emph{V-Dem v15} (Varieties of Democracy): Democracy indicators for
2000-2023 (Coppedge et al. 2011; Lindberg et al. 2014).

\emph{DPI 2020} (Database of Political Institutions): Government system
classifications (Cruz, Keefer, and Scartascini 2021).

\emph{QOG} (Quality of Government): Control variables including ethnic
fractionalization (Teorell et al. 2024).

\emph{World Bank WDI}: GDP per capita (PPP, constant 2017 USD) (World
Bank 2024).

\subsection{Variables}\label{variables}

\emph{Dependent Variable}: Democratic Volatility measures the standard
deviation of the V-Dem liberal democracy index for each country over
2000-2023. Higher values indicate greater instability in democratic
quality. Countries are required to have at least 20 years of non-missing
data.

\emph{Independent Variable}: Government System Type is coded as
Parliamentary (reference), Semi-Presidential, or Presidential based on
DPI classifications.

\emph{Moderator}: Log GDP per capita measures economic development using
the mean GDP per capita (PPP) over 2000-2023.

\emph{Controls}: The models include mean democracy level, ethnic
fractionalization, and a constitutional monarchy indicator.

\subsection{Analytical Strategy}\label{analytical-strategy}

OLS regression models with robust standard errors are estimated (R Core
Team 2024; Wickham et al. 2019):

\[Volatility_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 System_i + \beta_2 LogGDP_i + \beta_3 (System_i \times LogGDP_i) + \gamma X_i + \epsilon_i\]

The interaction term \(\beta_3\) captures the core theoretical claim
that system effects vary with development (Brambor, Clark, and Golder
2006).

\section{Case Studies: Mechanisms in Action}\label{sec-cases}

To illuminate the mechanisms underlying the statistical findings, three
illustrative cases are examined. Following Seawright and Gerring
(2008)'s typological case selection principles, the cases maximize
variation on both the key independent variable (system type) and
moderating variable (GDP level) while holding constant other factors
where possible. All three cases fall \emph{below} the crossover point of
\$14,000 GDP per capita, where presidential systems are theorized to
hold advantages. The cases are: (1) \textbf{India} (parliamentary,
\textasciitilde\$5,600 GDP), a potential deviant case where
parliamentary governance operates in a low-income context; (2)
\textbf{Ghana} (presidential, \textasciitilde\$5,000 GDP), a case
confirming presidential stability in a low-income African democracy; and
(3) \textbf{Indonesia} (presidential, \textasciitilde\$9,600 GDP), a
case of successful democratic consolidation under presidentialism at
moderate development. This selection enables both theory testing and
theory building.

\subsection{India: Parliamentary Governance in a Low-Income
Context}\label{india-parliamentary-governance-in-a-low-income-context}

India represents a crucial test case for the theoretical framework, as
it maintains parliamentary governance despite falling well below the
crossover point where parliamentary advantages are expected to emerge.
With GDP per capita around \$5,600, India operates in the zone where
presidential systems should theoretically perform better.

India's relatively high democratic volatility (0.114 in the sample)
partly supports this expectation. The country has experienced periods of
democratic stress, including the Emergency period (1975-1977) and more
recent concerns about democratic backsliding (Ganguly, DeVotta, and
Braroo 2020). The parliamentary system's dependence on coalition
politics has produced both stability through consensus-building and
instability through frequent government changes.

However, India also illustrates scope conditions for the theoretical
framework. India's robust institutional heritage, including an
independent judiciary and election commission, provides constraints that
typically emerge at higher development levels (Varshney 2013). This
suggests that historical institutional legacies can partially substitute
for the development-based mechanisms identified in this analysis.

\subsection{Ghana: Presidential Success in Low-Income
Africa}\label{ghana-presidential-success-in-low-income-africa}

Ghana exemplifies the presidential advantage in low-income contexts that
the statistical analysis predicts. With GDP per capita around \$5,000
and remarkably low democratic volatility (0.033), Ghana has achieved
democratic stability unusual for its income level and region
(Gyimah-Boadi 1999).

Ghana's presidential system has facilitated effective crisis management
through executive leadership, particularly during economic stabilization
periods. The concentration of authority in the presidency enabled
coherent policy responses to external shocks without the coalition
fragility that parliamentary systems might experience in weak-party
environments.

Importantly, Ghana's success also reflects party institutionalization.
The two-party system featuring the NPP and NDC provides structured
competition within the presidential framework (Riedl 2014). This
suggests that while presidential systems may hold advantages at low
income levels, these advantages are enhanced when combined with
reasonable party system development.

\subsection{Indonesia: Democratic Consolidation Under
Presidentialism}\label{indonesia-democratic-consolidation-under-presidentialism}

Indonesia provides compelling evidence for presidential effectiveness at
moderate development levels. With GDP per capita around \$9,600 (below
the crossover) and moderate volatility (0.054), Indonesia has
consolidated democracy following the 1998 transition from
authoritarianism.

Indonesia's presidential system enabled decisive leadership during the
post-Suharto transition while constitutional reforms introduced
legislative and judicial checks (Aspinall 2010). The direct presidential
elections created clear mandates for reform agendas, while the
multi-party legislature provided representation for Indonesia's diverse
population.

The Indonesian case demonstrates the crisis response mechanism at work.
Presidential authority facilitated economic stabilization after the
1997-98 Asian financial crisis and effective responses to subsequent
challenges. The flexibility to act decisively proved more valuable than
the consensus-building advantages parliamentary systems would provide at
higher development levels.

\subsection{Cross-Case Comparison}\label{cross-case-comparison}

These cases reveal important patterns. First, the cases confirm that at
low GDP levels, presidential systems can achieve democratic stability,
while parliamentary systems face challenges (India). Second, the success
cases (Ghana, Indonesia) demonstrate that presidential effectiveness at
low income levels is enhanced by complementary factors, including party
system development and institutional reforms. Third, the cases suggest
that historical institutional legacies can modify the basic system
type-development relationship, as India's parliamentary persistence
demonstrates.

The cases collectively support the theoretical framework while
highlighting scope conditions. Presidential systems appear advantaged at
low development, but this advantage is neither automatic nor guaranteed.
Complementary institutional features matter considerably.

\section{Discussion: Causal Interpretation and
Limitations}\label{sec-discussion}

\subsection{Addressing Endogeneity
Concerns}\label{addressing-endogeneity-concerns}

A fundamental challenge for institutional research is that government
systems are not randomly assigned. Countries may select systems based on
factors that also influence democratic stability, potentially biasing
our estimates (Cheibub 2007).

Several patterns mitigate this concern while not eliminating it.

\emph{Historical Timing}: Many current constitutional arrangements
reflect colonial legacies or critical junctures distant from
contemporary democracy levels. British colonies often adopted
parliamentary systems, while Latin American countries followed U.S.
presidential models---choices predating current development trajectories
(Stepan and Skach 1993).

\emph{Interaction Effects}: While selection bias may confound the main
effect of system type, it is less clear why selection would generate the
specific interaction pattern we observe. For selection to explain our
findings, countries would need to systematically choose presidential
systems based on factors that promote stability specifically at low
income levels and undermine it at high income levels.

\emph{Mechanism Evidence}: Our case studies provide process-tracing
evidence supporting the proposed mechanisms rather than alternative
explanations. The pattern of institutional constraint effectiveness
varying with development appears in qualitative accounts across diverse
contexts.

\subsection{Alternative Explanations}\label{alternative-explanations}

We consider several alternative explanations for our findings:

\emph{Reverse Causality}: Perhaps democratic instability causes
constitutional change rather than vice versa. However, government system
type is highly persistent; we observe minimal system switching within
our study period, making reverse causality unlikely to drive results.

\emph{Omitted Variables}: Regional effects, colonial history, or
cultural factors might explain both system choice and stability
patterns. Our robustness checks with regional fixed effects partially
address this concern, though unmeasured confounding remains possible.

\emph{Measurement}: Our dependent variable captures volatility in V-Dem
democracy scores, which may conflate genuine instability with
measurement error. However, V-Dem's expert-coded methodology with
uncertainty quantification (Pemstein, Meserve, and Melton 2010) and our
use of long-term standard deviations should mitigate transient
measurement noise.

\subsection{Toward Causal
Identification}\label{toward-causal-identification}

Future research could pursue stronger causal identification through
several strategies (Imai et al. 2011):

\emph{Instrumental Variables}: Historical determinants of system choice
(colonial origin, neighborhood effects) could serve as instruments if
they satisfy exclusion restrictions.

\emph{Regression Discontinuity}: Countries near decision thresholds
during constitutional moments might provide quasi-experimental
variation.

\emph{Difference-in-Differences}: The few cases of system change (e.g.,
Turkey's 2017 constitutional referendum) could enable within-country
comparisons, though small samples limit statistical power.

\emph{Mechanism-Based Approaches}: Rather than seeking the total effect
of system type, future work could focus on specific mechanisms
(institutional constraints, party strength) that are more amenable to
identification (Hainmueller, Mummolo, and Xu 2019).

\subsection{Policy Implications}\label{policy-implications}

These findings advance the comparative institutions literature in
several ways. First, they demonstrate that the long-standing debate over
presidential versus parliamentary systems has been asking the wrong
question (Mainwaring and Shugart 2018). Rather than asking which system
is ``better'' for democracy, the more productive question becomes: under
what conditions does each system perform better?

Second, the results support theories emphasizing the conditional nature
of institutional effects. The mechanisms identified here, including
crisis response capacity, institutional constraints, and party
institutionalization, operate differently across developmental contexts
(Kitschelt 2000; Gandhi 2019).

For constitutional designers in developing countries, these findings
suggest caution about importing parliamentary models from wealthy
democracies. Presidential systems may offer stability advantages in
low-income settings where executive autonomy aids crisis response and
party systems remain weak (Przeworski and Limongi 1997, 165; Treisman
2020, 244).

Conversely, as countries develop, strengthening horizontal
accountability mechanisms and party systems may be more important than
the choice of executive structure per se (Gibler and Randazzo 2011;
Bizzarro et al. 2018). Cases like Indonesia demonstrate that
presidential structures can support democratic development when
accompanied by effective institutional constraints and competitive party
systems (Scheppele 2018; McCoy, Rahman, and Somer 2018, 22--26).

For countries near the crossover point (\textasciitilde\$14,000 GDP per
capita), the findings suggest genuine institutional ambiguity. In these
contexts, attention to complementary institutions (judicial
independence, legislative capacity, party system development) may matter
more than the presidential-parliamentary choice itself (Fish 2006;
Issacharoff 2010). The rise of polarization as a driver of democratic
erosion (Orhan 2022; Arbatli and Rosenberg 2021) suggests that
institutional design alone cannot guarantee democratic resilience
without addressing underlying societal divisions (McCoy and Somer 2021).

\section{Conclusion}\label{conclusion}

This paper has demonstrated that the relationship between government
system type and democratic stability depends critically on economic
development. Presidential systems appear to offer stability advantages
in low-income contexts, while parliamentary systems perform better at
high income levels. The crossover point of approximately \$14,000 GDP
per capita suggests that many democratizing countries fall in a range
where the optimal choice is ambiguous.

The theoretical framework emphasizing crisis response capacity,
institutional constraints, and party institutionalization provides a
coherent explanation for why institutional effects vary with development
(Przeworski 2004; Boix 2011). Case evidence from India, Ghana, and
Indonesia illuminates these mechanisms in action, while extensive
robustness checks support the stability of the quantitative findings.

Importantly, this analysis has been transparent about causal
identification limitations inherent in cross-national institutional
research (King, Keohane, and Verba 1994). While the evidence is
consistent with system type affecting democratic stability conditional
on development, selection effects and omitted variable bias cannot be
definitively ruled out (Cheibub and Limongi 2002, 151). Future research
using quasi-experimental designs or more granular mechanism-focused
approaches could strengthen causal claims (Imai et al. 2011).

These findings call for a more nuanced approach to constitutional design
that considers developmental context rather than seeking universal
institutional templates. As democratic backsliding threatens countries
at all income levels (Lührmann and Lindberg 2019, 1105; Haggard and
Kaufman 2021b), understanding the conditional effects of institutions on
democratic resilience has never been more important. The ongoing ``third
wave of autocratization'' (Gerschewski 2021) demands that scholars and
policymakers move beyond simple institutional prescriptions toward
contextually sensitive approaches that account for the complex interplay
between institutions, development, and democratic survival (Luo and
Przeworski 2023; Svolik 2019).
